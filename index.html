Main Topic

Life in Kamakura-Period Japan

The Kamakura period marked a major shift from court-centred rule toward a military government led by warriors.

侍

A changing government

Who held power?

The emperor and imperial court remained important symbols, but political and military authority increasingly rested with the shogunate. The shogun governed through warrior families, while the Hōjō clan later exercised great influence through the office of regent.

Most people were not warriors. Farmers produced food and paid dues, artisans created necessary goods, merchants exchanged products, and religious communities shaped education, belief and public life.

Key idea Military rule did not erase the imperial court.

Japan had overlapping centres of authority: the court in Kyoto and the warrior government in Kamakura.

Subtopic 1

The Mongol Invasions of Japan

Kublai Khan's forces attacked in 1274 and 1281. Japan's defenders faced unfamiliar tactics, large fleets and enormous pressure.

波

Interactive comparison

1274 or 1281?

Switch between the two invasion attempts.

First invasion 1274

The Bun'ei Campaign

The invading force attacked Tsushima and Iki before landing near Hakata Bay. Japanese defenders encountered coordinated formations, massed archery and gunpowder weapons.

Japanese preparation
Limited experience against a large overseas invasion
Important development
The defenders learned how different the attackers' tactics were
Outcome
The invading fleet withdrew after fighting and severe weather

Cause and response

From warning to invasion

Kublai Khan's government sent demands that Japan submit. The shogunate refused.

Beyond the storm

Victory had a cost

The storms became a powerful part of the invasions' memory, but Japanese resistance and preparations were also important. Defenders constructed fortifications, maintained readiness and fought from shore and small boats.

Think carefully A defensive victory produced little captured land.

Without new territory or spoils, rewarding warriors became difficult, contributing to dissatisfaction with the shogunate.
